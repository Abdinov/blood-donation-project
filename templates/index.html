Give Blood, Save Lives!

According to the University of Chicago Medicine , about one in seven people entering a hospital needs blood, and blood is needed every two seconds. More than 4.5 million Americans will need blood transfusion each year. It cannot be manufactured or replaced with a different substance. Individuals who are accident victims, surgery patients, hemophiliacs, or cancer patients depend on a sufficient supply of blood in hospitals. Donating blood is a moral duty of every citizen, especially because Only 37 percent of the U.S. population is eligible to donate blood — less than 10 percent do it annually. Those who can donate can save lives that otherwise will be lost to a depleted blood supply.

Donating blood can have both positive emotional and physical effects on the donor. A blood donor makes an act of altruism, which can ultimately help to save a patient's life. According to a report by the Mental Health Foundation, altruism can help to:

  • Reduce stress
  • Improve your emotional well-being
  • Benefit your physical health
  • Help get rid of negative feelings
  • Provide a sense of belonging and reduce isolation

Additionally, giving blood can reduce harmful iron stores in people who suffer hemochromatosis. One in every two hundred people in the U.S. is affected by this condition which causes an iron overload. High levels of iron can lead to cancer, heart attacks, or problems associated with the liver; donating blood can help to prevent all of the following diseases caused by high iron levels. Another benefit of donating your blood is getting your heart into better shape. Donating lowers the viscosity of your blood which reduces the friction on your arteries and blood vessels. Watch an interview with David DeRose, MD, to learn more about the benefits of blood donation.

In November 2019, the Red Cross issued an immediate call for blood donations due to a severe blood shortage. This is not the first and not the last time. At one point, when the COVID-19 pandemic was at its peak in the U.S., 33 percent of hospitals had just a day's supply of blood on their shelves to handle the needs of their patients and zero hospitals have it for more than three days. To learn more about the shortages during COVID-19 pandemic, you can watch the video below.

The fundamental reason for the crisis is not just a decline in the frequency of donors. There has been a decline in the number of donors too. What started as a civic duty-driven phenomenon, has changed into more of an "individual transformational impact". Individuals simply do not want to donate when the incentive is not direct; donors want to know exactly how and whom they helped. The industry is also seeing a decline because there isn't enough awareness being created to reach out to and keep existing younger donors, the millennials. This is why you need to become a donor.

What to do before donating blood?
  • Select a donation type and find a convenient time that works best for you.
  • Have iron-rich foods, such as red meat, fish, poultry, beans, spinach, iron-fortified cereals, or raisins
  • Get a good night's sleep the night before your donation, eat healthy foods and drink extra liquids
  • Avoid exercise or strenuous activities
What to do after donating blood?
  • Stay in a refreshments area for 15-20 minutes and enjoy a cool drink and savory snack
  • Drink plenty of fluids
  • Don’t stand for long periods
  • Try not to overheat
  • Avoid exercise or strenuous activities
  • Avoid alcohol
  • Avoid strenuous exercise
Are there any risks associated with donating blood?

Some of the side effects of donating blood include:

  • Bruising
  • Dizziness, lightheadedness, and nausea
  • Physical weakness
  • Pain in the arm

However, all of the above side effects are temporary and fade away after some time. These side effects are nothing compared to the good that's being done by donating blood.
